# Black Pepper Pest and Disease Guide

## Overview
Black pepper (Piper nigrum) is a high-value spice crop grown primarily in Kerala, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu. Phytophthora quick wilt is the most devastating disease — it can kill entire vines within weeks and has caused massive losses in Kerala's pepper gardens. Integrated disease management combining cultural practices, biocontrol, and judicious use of fungicides is essential.

---

## Pest and Disease Entries

---

### Phytophthora Foot Rot / Quick Wilt (Phytophthora capsici)

**Type:** Fungal (Oomycete)

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden wilting of entire vine within days to weeks — "quick wilt"
- Water-soaked lesions at collar region (base of vine near soil)
- Dark brown to black rotting of roots and basal stem
- Leaves turn yellow and fall rapidly — bare vine
- Berries blacken and fall prematurely
- Soil splash during rain spreads spores to leaves — brown lesions on leaves and spikes

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Heavy continuous rainfall — especially July–September monsoon
- Poorly drained, waterlogged soils
- Temperature: 20–30°C
- Disease spreads rapidly through field during monsoon via irrigation and runoff

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Metalaxyl + Mancozeb (Ridomil Gold) — 2.5 g/litre soil drench around vine base
- Foliar spray of Bordeaux mixture 1% on leaves and spikes
- Copper hydroxide (Kocide) 3 g/litre as drench and spray

**Organic Alternative:**
- Trichoderma harzianum — 50 g per vine as soil application before monsoon
- Pseudomonas fluorescens — 20 g per vine soil application
- Bordeaux mixture 1% preventive spray before monsoon begins

**Prevention:**
- Ensure excellent drainage in pepper garden — do not allow waterlogging ever
- Apply Bordeaux mixture 1% spray in May (before monsoon) as preventive
- Remove and destroy badly infected vines with surrounding soil
- Do not replant in same spot for 2–3 years
- Apply organic mulch around vine base to prevent soil splash
- Phytosanitation — clean tools with 1% bleach between vines

### Pollu Beetle (Longitarsus nigripennis)

**Type:** Insect (Coleoptera)

**Symptoms:**
- Small round holes bored into pepper berries
- Grubs inside berries — berries turn black and fall prematurely
- Infested berries are hollow and shriveled
- Berries drop well before maturity causing severe yield loss
- Adult beetles are tiny metallic blue-black, visible on spikes

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Season: September–November when berries are forming
- Humid conditions in dense pepper gardens
- Gardens with shady and humid microclimate

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Quinalphos 25% EC — 2 ml/litre spray on spikes and berries
- Application method: Spray on berry clusters at 15-day intervals during September–November

**Organic Alternative:**
- Neem oil 5 ml/litre spray
- Kaolin particle film spray creates physical barrier

**Prevention:**
- Collect and destroy fallen infested berries daily
- Prune to reduce canopy density and improve air circulation
- Timely harvest to reduce exposure period

### Anthracnose / Colletotrichum Dieback (Colletotrichum gloeosporioides)

**Type:** Fungal

**Symptoms:**
- Dark brown to black lesions on stems causing dieback from tip downward
- Circular water-soaked spots on leaves turning brown with yellow halo
- Infected berries show sunken dark spots and fall prematurely
- New shoot tips die back — stag-head appearance on branches
- Salmon-colored spore masses on wet lesions

**Triggering Conditions:**
- High humidity and frequent rain
- Season: Monsoon July–September
- Wounds from pruning, insects, or mechanical damage are entry points

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Carbendazim 50% WP — 1 g/litre spray on affected parts
- Alternatively: Propiconazole 25% EC — 1 ml/litre

**Organic Alternative:**
- Copper oxychloride 3 g/litre spray
- Trichoderma viride soil application near vine base

**Prevention:**
- Prune with sterilized tools — dip in 1% bleach between cuts
- Apply Bordeaux paste on pruning cuts
- Maintain vigor through balanced nutrition — potassium is critical for disease resistance

### Mosaic Virus (Pepper Veinal Mottle Virus — PVMV)

**Type:** Viral

**Symptoms:**
- Mosaic pattern of light and dark green on leaves
- Leaves are smaller, distorted, and puckered
- Yellow vein banding visible on leaves held to light
- Stunted growth; reduced flowering and berry set
- No cure once infected

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Transmitted by aphids (Myzus persicae, Aphis gossypii)
- Any season when aphid populations are high
- New plantings near infected older gardens are at high risk

**Treatment:**
- No treatment — remove and destroy infected vines
- Control aphid vector with Imidacloprid 17.8% SL — 0.5 ml/litre

**Organic Alternative:**
- Neem oil spray to reduce aphid vector population
- Reflective silver mulch disorients aphids

**Prevention:**
- Use certified virus-free planting material (rooted cuttings)
- Control aphid vectors in young gardens
- Do not plant near old infected gardens
- Roguing — immediately remove any mosaic-symptomatic vine
