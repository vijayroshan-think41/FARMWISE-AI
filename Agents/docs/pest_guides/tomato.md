# Tomato Pest and Disease Guide

## Overview
Tomato is one of the most economically important vegetables in India, grown across Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu. It is highly susceptible to fungal diseases, viral infections, and insect pests — especially under humid conditions or poor crop rotation. Early identification and prompt action are critical to protect yield and quality.

---

## Pest and Disease Entries

---

### Early Blight (Alternaria solani)

**Type:** Fungal

**Symptoms:**
- Dark brown to black circular spots with concentric rings (target-board pattern) on older leaves
- Yellow halo surrounding the spots
- Leaves turn yellow and drop prematurely
- Stem lesions at soil level in seedlings (damping-off)
- Dark sunken spots on fruit near stem end

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 24–29°C
- Humidity: Above 70%
- Season: Most common in Kharif (September–October) and post-monsoon
- Favored by alternating wet and dry periods

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Mancozeb 75% WP — 2 g/litre, spray every 7–10 days
- Alternatively: Chlorothalonil 75% WP — 2 g/litre
- Application method: Foliar spray covering both leaf surfaces

**Organic Alternative:**
- Copper oxychloride 50% WP — 3 g/litre spray
- Neem oil 5 ml/litre + soap 1 ml/litre as spreader

**Prevention:**
- Use certified disease-free seed
- Avoid overhead irrigation — use drip
- Remove and destroy infected plant debris
- Maintain crop rotation — avoid tomato after tomato or potato
- Apply mulch to reduce soil splash on leaves

### Late Blight (Phytophthora infestans)

**Type:** Fungal (Oomycete)

**Symptoms:**
- Water-soaked pale green to brown patches on leaves, rapidly enlarging
- White cottony growth on underside of leaves during humid mornings
- Dark brown lesions on stems
- Fruit shows firm brown patches that spread quickly
- Entire field can collapse within 7–10 days in cool humid weather

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 10–20°C (cool conditions)
- Humidity: Above 90%
- Season: October–December in Nashik and Deccan; December–February in hills
- Spreads very fast during foggy or rainy spells

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Metalaxyl + Mancozeb (Ridomil Gold) — 2.5 g/litre, spray every 5–7 days
- Alternatively: Cymoxanil + Mancozeb — 2 g/litre
- Application method: Foliar spray — begin preventively before disease appears in high-risk weather

**Organic Alternative:**
- Copper hydroxide (Kocide) — 3 g/litre
- Bordeaux mixture 1% — spray fortnightly as preventive

**Prevention:**
- Plant resistant varieties (Arka Rakshak, Pusa Hybrid-1)
- Avoid planting in low-lying areas with poor drainage
- Destroy volunteer tomato plants
- Monitor weather forecasts — spray before rain forecast
- Space plants adequately for air circulation

### Tomato Mosaic Virus (ToMV)

**Type:** Viral

**Symptoms:**
- Mosaic pattern of light and dark green on leaves
- Leaves are malformed, smaller than normal, curled inward
- Stunted plant growth
- Fruits show yellow blotches or internal browning
- No treatment possible once infected

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Transmitted by contact (tools, hands) and seed
- Not transmitted by insects (unlike other viruses)
- Any season; worse in dense plantings

**Treatment:**
- No cure — infected plants must be removed and destroyed immediately
- Disinfect tools with 1% sodium hypochlorite between plants

**Organic Alternative:**
- No organic treatment — prevention only

**Prevention:**
- Use certified virus-free seed or treated seed
- Wash hands before handling plants
- Do not use tobacco near tomato plants
- Remove and burn infected plants immediately
- Avoid replanting tomato in infected field for 2 seasons

### Whitefly (Bemisia tabaci)

**Type:** Insect

**Symptoms:**
- Tiny white flying insects on underside of leaves
- Leaves turn yellow, curl upward
- Sticky honeydew on leaf surface causing sooty mold (black coating)
- Whitefly is the primary vector for Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus (TYLCV)
- Severe infestations cause plant wilting and fruit drop

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 25–35°C
- Humidity: Low to moderate (dry conditions favor population buildup)
- Season: Kharif and Rabi both; peak in November–January
- Dense planting and dust on leaves worsen infestation

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Imidacloprid 17.8% SL — 0.5 ml/litre, spray on leaf undersides
- Alternatively: Thiamethoxam 25% WG — 0.3 g/litre
- Application method: Direct spray on leaf undersides; rotate insecticides to prevent resistance

**Organic Alternative:**
- Neem oil 5 ml/litre + garlic extract 5 ml/litre spray
- Yellow sticky traps — 10 per acre to monitor and reduce population
- Reflective silver mulch repels whitefly

**Prevention:**
- Install 40-mesh nylon net in nursery
- Remove heavily infested leaves and destroy
- Plant marigold border rows as trap crop
- Avoid excess nitrogen fertilization which attracts whitefly
- Monitor weekly with yellow sticky traps

### Fruit Borer (Helicoverpa armigera)

**Type:** Insect (Lepidoptera)

**Symptoms:**
- Small circular entry holes on fruit surface
- Caterpillar found inside fruit feeding on seeds and pulp
- Frass (excrement) visible around entry hole
- Damaged fruits rot quickly due to secondary infection
- Young larvae initially feed on leaves and flowers before entering fruit

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 25–35°C
- Season: Active throughout growing season; peak in September–November
- High nitrogen fertilization increases susceptibility
- Continuous tomato cultivation increases moth population

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Spinosad 45% SC — 0.3 ml/litre spray on fruits and foliage
- Alternatively: Chlorantraniliprole (Coragen) — 0.3 ml/litre
- Application method: Foliar spray covering fruits thoroughly; spray in evening

**Organic Alternative:**
- Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) — 2 g/litre spray at egg hatching stage
- Neem seed kernel extract (NSKE) 5% spray
- Pheromone traps — 5 traps per acre to monitor adult moth

**Prevention:**
- Install pheromone traps from transplanting onwards
- Remove and destroy damaged fruits immediately
- Deep summer plowing to kill pupae in soil
- Avoid mono-cropping of tomato for multiple seasons
- Intercrop with coriander or basil to attract natural enemies
