# Wheat Pest and Disease Guide

## Overview
Wheat is the primary Rabi crop across Punjab, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, and Madhya Pradesh. The crop faces serious threats from rust diseases, smut, and insect pests. Punjab and Haryana are considered the wheat bowl of India — disease outbreaks here have national food security implications. Early season monitoring from January onwards is critical.

## Pest and Disease Entries

---

### Yellow Rust (Puccinia striiformis)

**Type:** Fungal

**Symptoms:**
- Yellow to orange pustules arranged in stripes along leaf veins
- Powdery yellow-orange spores rub off on fingers
- Leaves turn yellow then brown and dry
- Early infection at flag leaf stage is most damaging
- Spreads rapidly in cool weather — field can be fully infected in 10 days

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 10–15°C (cool weather is critical trigger)
- Humidity: High; dew and foggy mornings
- Season: January–March in Punjab, December–February in hills
- New virulent races (78S84, 46S119) overcome resistant varieties

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Propiconazole 25% EC — 1 ml/litre, spray immediately at first sign
- Alternatively: Tebuconazole 25.9% EC — 1 ml/litre
- Application method: Foliar spray; repeat after 10–14 days if conditions persist

**Organic Alternative:**
- No effective organic treatment — act fast with chemical
- Sulfur dust 25 kg/ha can slow spread slightly

**Prevention:**
- Use rust-resistant varieties (HD-3086, WB02, PBW-343 with Yr genes)
- Monitor fields weekly from January in Punjab
- Avoid late sowing which extends crop duration into rust season
- Do not over-irrigate — wet canopy favors spore germination

### Brown Rust (Puccinia recondita)

**Type:** Fungal

**Symptoms:**
- Small round orange-brown pustules scattered randomly on leaves (not in stripes)
- Brown powdery spores rub off on touch
- Severely affected leaves dry and die
- Usually appears after yellow rust season — February to March
- Less damaging than yellow rust but can cause 20–30% yield loss

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 15–22°C (warmer than yellow rust)
- Humidity: Moderate to high
- Season: February–March peak in plains

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Propiconazole 25% EC — 1 ml/litre spray
- Application method: Foliar spray at first pustule appearance

**Organic Alternative:**
- Neem oil 5 ml/litre has limited effectiveness — use only for very mild infections

**Prevention:**
- Same as yellow rust — resistant varieties and timely sowing
- Ensure balanced potassium nutrition which improves rust tolerance

### Loose Smut (Ustilago tritici)

**Type:** Fungal (Seed-borne)

**Symptoms:**
- Entire ear replaced by black powdery spore mass
- Black smutted heads visible above healthy crop at heading
- Spore mass disperses rapidly at flowering — infects neighboring plants
- Affected plants are taller and head earlier than healthy plants
- No grain formed in infected heads

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Spread during flowering — wind-borne spores infect open flowers
- Cool, humid conditions at flowering favor spore germination
- Seed-borne — infected seed carries the fungus inside the embryo

**Treatment:**
- No field treatment possible — disease is inside the plant
- Rogue out and destroy infected heads before spores disperse (bag and remove)

**Organic Alternative:**
- Hot water seed treatment — soak seed in hot water at 52°C for 10 minutes then dry

**Prevention:**
- Use certified disease-free seed
- Seed treatment with Carboxin 37.5% + Thiram 37.5% (Vitavax) — 2.5 g/kg seed
- Biproxy (Tebuconazole 2% DS) seed treatment — 1.5 g/kg seed
- Do not save seed from fields with smut infection

### Aphids (Sitobion avenae — Grain Aphid)

**Type:** Insect (Hemiptera)

**Symptoms:**
- Clusters of small green to brown soft-bodied insects on leaves, stem, and ear
- Sticky honeydew on plant surface causing sooty mold
- Leaves turn yellow; severe infestations cause plant wilting
- Aphids on ears reduce grain filling — shriveled grain
- Economic threshold: 50 aphids per tiller at tillering; 200 per ear at heading

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 15–25°C
- Season: December–March in Rabi wheat
- Dry weather and warm spells in February–March cause rapid population buildup
- Excess nitrogen fertilization attracts aphids

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Imidacloprid 17.8% SL — 0.5 ml/litre spray
- Alternatively: Dimethoate 30% EC (Rogor) — 2 ml/litre
- Application method: Foliar spray; treat only when threshold exceeded to protect natural enemies

**Organic Alternative:**
- Neem oil 5 ml/litre + soap 1 ml/litre
- Spray water jet to dislodge colonies
- Protect natural enemies — ladybirds, lacewings, parasitic wasps

**Prevention:**
- Timely sowing (November 1–15) to avoid peak aphid season
- Balanced fertilization — avoid excess nitrogen
- Do not over-irrigate — high humidity favors aphids
- Monitor weekly from December onwards

### Termites (Odontotermes spp.)

**Type:** Insect (Isoptera)

**Symptoms:**
- Plants wilt suddenly and pull out easily from soil
- Root and stem base eaten — hollowed out with mud-filled galleries
- Affected plants appear in patches in the field
- More common in sandy soils and fields with undecomposed organic matter
- Problem worsens in dry soils

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Dry soil conditions — termites avoid waterlogged soil
- Sandy soils with low clay content
- Fields with crop stubble or roots left undecomposed
- Season: Most active in dry November–December after sowing

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Chlorpyrifos 20% EC — 2.5 litre/ha dissolved in irrigation water (drench)
- Alternatively: Imidacloprid 600 FS seed treatment — 6 ml/kg seed before sowing

**Organic Alternative:**
- Neem cake 250 kg/ha incorporated in soil before sowing
- Bacillus cereus biocontrol application

**Prevention:**
- Deep summer plowing to destroy termite colonies
- Remove all crop stubble and roots before sowing
- Avoid applying raw fresh manure or green material — use well-composted FYM only
- Seed treatment with chlorpyrifos or imidacloprid before sowing
