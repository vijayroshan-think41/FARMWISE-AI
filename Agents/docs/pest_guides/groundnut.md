# Groundnut Pest and Disease Guide

## Overview
Groundnut (peanut) is a major oilseed and cash crop in Andhra Pradesh, Gujarat, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Rajasthan. It is particularly vulnerable to Tikka leaf spot and collar rot in humid conditions. Aflatoxin contamination from Aspergillus infection in storage is a serious food safety concern that must be prevented through proper drying and storage.

## Pest and Disease Entries

---

### Tikka Disease — Early and Late Leaf Spot (Cercospora arachidicola & Phaeoisariopsis personata)

**Type:** Fungal

**Symptoms:**
- Early leaf spot: Circular brown spots with yellow halo on upper leaf surface; white/gray growth on underside
- Late leaf spot: Dark brown to black circular spots, no yellow halo; more numerous
- Severely infected leaves drop prematurely causing defoliation
- Defoliation reduces pod fill significantly — 50–70% yield loss if uncontrolled
- Both diseases appear together; late leaf spot is more aggressive

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 25–30°C
- Humidity: Above 70%; prolonged leaf wetness
- Season: Kharif — August to September peak

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Chlorothalonil 75% WP — 2 g/litre spray every 10 days from 30 DAS
- Alternatively: Mancozeb 75% WP — 2.5 g/litre
- Application method: Foliar spray — begin preventively at 30 DAS

**Organic Alternative:**
- Neem oil 5 ml/litre spray
- Sulfur 80% WP — 3 g/litre spray

**Prevention:**
- Use resistant varieties (TAG-24, ICGV-86031)
- Apply gypsum 500 kg/ha at pegging to improve calcium nutrition
- Maintain crop rotation — avoid groundnut after groundnut
- Remove and plow under crop debris

### Collar Rot (Aspergillus niger)

**Type:** Fungal

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden wilting of seedlings at collar region (soil surface)
- Blackening and rotting of stem at soil level
- Black sclerotia visible on rotted tissue
- Seedlings pull out easily — roots completely rotted
- Patches of dead seedlings in field

**Triggering Conditions:**
- High soil moisture immediately after sowing
- Poor drainage; heavy clay soils
- Seed sown too deep or seed coat damaged
- Season: Early Kharif after heavy rains

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Carbendazim drench at 1 g/litre around affected plants
- Remove and destroy infected plants to prevent spread

**Organic Alternative:**
- Trichoderma viride seed treatment — 4 g/kg seed before sowing

**Prevention:**
- Seed treatment with Thiram 75% WS + Carbendazim 50% WP — 3 g/kg seed
- Ensure good drainage in field
- Avoid over-irrigation at germination stage
- Sow at optimum depth (5 cm) — not too shallow or deep

### Aphids (Aphis craccivora — Groundnut Aphid)

**Type:** Insect

**Symptoms:**
- Clusters of small black aphids on growing tips and young leaves
- Curling and puckering of young leaves
- Stunted shoot growth
- Sticky honeydew causing sooty mold
- Aphids transmit Groundnut Rosette Virus — most damaging viral disease of groundnut in Africa

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Temperature: 20–30°C
- Season: Early Kharif — June to July peak when crop is young
- Dry weather with moderate temperature
- Dense planting increases aphid buildup

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Imidacloprid 17.8% SL — 0.5 ml/litre spray
- Application method: Foliar spray on growing tips; use only when threshold crossed (50 aphids per plant)

**Organic Alternative:**
- Neem oil 5 ml/litre spray every 5 days
- Soap solution 10 ml/litre to suffocate colonies

**Prevention:**
- Seed treatment with Imidacloprid 600 FS — 5 ml/kg seed
- Maintain plant vigor through balanced fertilization
- Avoid planting near older infected crops

### White Grub (Holotrichia spp.)

**Type:** Insect (Coleoptera)

**Symptoms:**
- Patches of wilted plants — pull out easily with no roots attached
- Creamy-white C-shaped grubs found in soil around roots
- Roots and peg zone completely eaten
- Pods show feeding damage — holes and missing sections
- Problem is underground and not visible until severe damage done

**Triggering Conditions:**
- Sandy soils with low clay content
- Season: Kharif — grubs active June to October
- Adult beetles emerge during May–June rains and lay eggs in soil

**Treatment:**
- Primary chemical: Chlorpyrifos 20% EC — 5 litre/ha in irrigation water as drench
- Alternatively: Phorate 10G — 10 kg/ha applied in furrow at sowing

**Organic Alternative:**
- Entomopathogenic nematodes (Heterorhabditis spp.) — soil application
- Metarhizium anisopliae fungus — soil application 2.5 kg/ha

**Prevention:**
- Deep summer plowing to expose and kill grubs and pupae
- Light traps to mass-catch adult beetles during May–June emergence
- Seed treatment with Imidacloprid or Chlorpyrifos
- Avoid applying fresh farmyard manure which attracts egg-laying beetles
